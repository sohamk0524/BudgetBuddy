Across the 10 customer interviews, we found that budgeting is not structured for most students. Most students avoid tools, choosing to budget mentally or by using spending warnings from their bank itself. In general, we noticed the core issue isn’t tracking expenses itself but rather making budgeting automated, visual, and more supportive around one’s schedule.

  
We first believed that the issue was tracking expenses, and wanted to build a tool around that. However, these customer interviews showed us that the root cause of budgeting problems is a lack of proactive visibility, emotional stress, and social pressures (e.g. students go out with friends and find it hard to say no).

## 1. Manual tracking and budgeting is time-consuming and unintuitive


Students that used spreadsheets noted that they found it painful to
- Consolidate multiple accounts
- Enter transaction details manually
- Categorize transactions themselves
- Get visual feedback

In general, manual tracking isn’t sustainable, especially for students who are earning money for the first time. Students want more visual feedback and automation.

## 2. Most students mentally track their expenses

Across the customer interviews, the majority of students track spending in their head, setting rough limits and adjusting their spending as they check their bank account.

They still note that even when mentally tracking, they overspend by accident, typically because of small expenses that accumulate. And, they tend to reach their limit before the end of the month.

Key issues with mentally tracking are that it becomes difficult to gauge how close you are to your limit with small expenses that add up and this can lead to accidental overspending.

  

## 3. Students spend socially and out of convenience

Common themes of spending include

- Students spending too much money on eating out with friends, and find it hard to say no

- Unexpected costs accumulate (e.g. for a trip you have to account for transportation, parking, etc)

- Students spend more out of convenience (e.g. spending money on eating out when they have midterms that week)

We know that BudgetBuddy should be flexible when it comes to eating out with friends, since a lot of students face that social pressure. And, BudgetBuddy might be useful for keeping students proactively aware of potential costs. For example, if students go to Sac just for food they also might find themselves spending money on dessert even if they don’t plan for it. We can help students be prepared for these costs and even find more budget-friendly alternatives.

  

## 4. Current budgeting apps feel like too much

Students that have considered but not used budgeting apps generally claim

- There are many apps out there and it’s difficult to pick one

- Apps don’t unify across multiple accounts

- They are concerned about security

Students generally note that they want

- One app for everything

- Visual feedback on their spending/budgeting goals

- Clear, color-coded categories

- Notifications without needing to open the app

- Proactive feedback

- Weekly/daily breakdowns

Generally, students want as much of the process to be automated and visual as possible. They want to understand their spending and have the app notify them when they are about to overspend rather than put the pressure on students consciously spending less. They also want apps to be friendly towards users with little financial knowledge, as students have typically not had experience with financial planning or budgeting before.

  

## Summary

Before, we thought that the main problem was that students needed a way to track expenses so they could budget for themselves. Here are some key insights we found after customer interviews.

Key triggers that lead to overspending:

- Students spend out of social pressure

- Students forget to account for unexpected costs

- Small expenses accumulate without students being aware (e.g. coffee, travel, etc.)

Key areas we can solve these problems

- Give visual feedback (e.g. charts, breakdowns)

- Give proactive warnings, rather than after overspending happens

- Beginner-friendly

- Give frequent reminders without having to actually go into the app

- Account for students schedules (e.g. exams, holidays, etc.)
